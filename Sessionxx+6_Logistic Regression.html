Objectives of Logistic Regression Models

  • Logistic regression (Logit) is similar to linear regression such as ordinary least squares (OLS)

    • Estimate the relationship between independent variables X and an independent variable Y

  • The major difference is Logit is used when Y is categorical

    • In this class, we only consider Y can have two possible categories, i.e., binary

  • The objective of logistic regression is to determine how one or more independent variables X impact the probability that an observation falls into one of the two categories captured by the y variable

Common Uses of Logistic Regression Models

  • Logistic regression is commonly used by Marketing researchers.

    • Customer acquisition: Will a certain customer buy the company’s product (yes/no)?

    • Predict retention of current customers (yes/no)

      • Which customers are most likely to defect considering certain predictor (x) variables?

    • Estimate the likelihood that a person will respond favorably to a mailing campaign (yes/no)

Logistic Regression Model

  • The logit model takes the following form

\[ p=\frac{e^{\beta_{0}+\beta_{1}x_{1}+\beta_{2}x_{2}+\beta_{3}x_{3}...}}{1+e^{\beta_{0}+\beta_{1}x_{1}+\beta_{2}x_{2}+\beta_{3}x_{3}...}} \]where

  • \(e\) is the base of the natural logarithm, i.e., 2.71828,

  • \(p\) is the probability of happening (e.g., probability of customer churn)

  • \(x\) are the predictor variables, and \(\beta\) are the coefficients to be estimated

  • The model estimates the probability of the event happening (e.g., churn) as a function of the \(X\) variables.

Logistic Regression Model

  • \(p\) is the probability: \(p\) will be between 0 and 1

  • Logistic regression can be performed with one or more x variables that can be either continuous of binary.

  • With only one x variable, the probability p follows an s-shape

Odds of an event

  • The probability can be translated to odds of an event

\[ \frac{p}{1-p}=e^{\beta_{0}+\beta_{1}x_{1}+\beta_{2}x_{2}+\beta_{3}x_{3}...} \]

  • Probability ranges between 0 and 1, where 0 is an impossible event and 1 an inevitable event

  • Odds on the other hand can range from 0 to infinity. Odds capture the ratio of how often an event of interest occurred (e.g., customers who churned) over how often it did not occur (e.g., customers who did not churn)

Model Fit and Testing for Significance of the Coefficients

  • Logistic regression uses maximum likelihood to estimate the regression coefficients

    • the algorithm maximizes the likelihood that an event occurs using iterative computing methods.

    • logistic regression model fit is also assessed differently

  • This means that the R-squared is hard to obtain

  • Instead, R reports information criteria, called AIC

  • A lower AIC value means a better fit

Breakeven Analysis

  • To decide whom we want to target, we need to identify a break-even rate

  • We need the following information

    • Average purchase amounts from the marketing campaign: $40

    • Average cost of good sold (contribution margin): $19.20

    • Average cost of shipping: $6

    • Average cost of marketing campaign (e.g., catalog): $2

  • Break-even rate is

    \[ \text{breakeven}=\frac{\text{cost of marketing campaign}}{\text{Profit from a single sale}}=\frac{\$2}{\$12.8}=15.63\% \]

  • Let’s figure out whom to target using R

Takeaways

  • Logistic regression is akin to other regression models (e.g. OLS regression) but is used when the dependent (i.e., outcome) variable is binary instead of, for example, continuous.

  • Logistic regression allows researchers to estimate the probability that a binary outcome of interest occurs based on one or more predictor variables. 

  • Logistic regression allows researchers to identify variables that significantly predict the binary outcome variable.

  • The probability with which a binary event of interest occurs based on predictor variables can also be expressed in odds. 

Takeaways

  • Probability and odds are often used interchangeably, but they are not equivalent mathematically. Probability captures the ratio of how often a binary event of interest occurs (e.g., customers who churn) over all possible events (e.g., customers who do and do not churn). 

  • In contrast, odds capture the ratio of how often an event of interest occurs (e.g., customers who churn) over how often it does not occur (e.g., customer who do not churn).
